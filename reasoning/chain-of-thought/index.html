    Chain of Thought Reasoner¶

    The Chain of Thought reasoner breaks down complex problems into sequential steps, explicitly tracking the line of reasoning that leads to a solution. By making each logical step visible, it creates a transparent reasoning process that's easy to follow, validate, and debug.

    Ideal for Step-by-Step Problem Solving

    Chain of Thought excels at problems with clear logical structure that benefit from methodical decomposition, such as mathematical calculations, logical deductions, or multi-stage analyses.

    Key Features¶

    • Sequential reasoning - Breaks problems into ordered steps
    • Explicit intermediate conclusions - Captures key insights at each stage
    • Structured thought process - Creates clear, traceable reasoning paths
    • Error detection - Makes it easier to identify flawed assumptions or calculation errors
    • Self-correction - Can revise earlier steps when needed

    When to Use¶

    Chain of Thought reasoning is particularly effective for:

    • Mathematical or quantitative problems
    • Multi-step logical deductions
    • Planning tasks requiring sequential steps
    • Debugging thought processes
    • Explaining complex reasoning to others

    Basic Usage¶

    import { createNeuroCore } from 'neucore';
import { ReasoningMethod } from 'neucore/reasoning';

const core = createNeuroCore();

// Using the Chain of Thought reasoner
const result = await core.reasoning.solve({
  problem: "A store has a 25% off sale. If an item originally costs $120, what is the final price after applying a 10% coupon to the already discounted price?",
  method: ReasoningMethod.CHAIN_OF_THOUGHT,
  options: {
    temperature: 0.1 // Lower temperature for more deterministic reasoning
  }
});

console.log(result.solution); // Final answer
console.log(result.steps); // Intermediate steps in the reasoning process

    Advanced Configuration¶

    The Chain of Thought reasoner supports several configuration options:

    const result = await core.reasoning.solve({
  problem: "What would be the environmental impact if all passenger cars in the US switched to electric by 2030?",
  method: ReasoningMethod.CHAIN_OF_THOUGHT,
  options: {
    temperature: 0.2,
    methodOptions: {
      model: "gpt-4o",                // Specific model to use
      maxSteps: 10,                   // Maximum number of reasoning steps
      verifySteps: true,              // Double-check each step for errors
      explicitAssumptions: true,      // Clearly state assumptions made
      exploreAlternatives: true,      // Consider different approaches
      quantifyUncertainty: true,      // Acknowledge confidence levels
      citeSources: false,             // Don't require source citations
      detailedExplanations: true      // Provide thorough explanations
    }
  }
});

    Implementation Details¶

    Reasoning Graph Structure¶

    The Chain of Thought reasoner produces a structured reasoning graph with:

    1. The problem statement node (type: ReasoningNodeType.QUESTION)
    2. Multiple reasoning step nodes (type: ReasoningNodeType.INFERENCE)
    3. A solution node with the final answer (type: ReasoningNodeType.INFERENCE)

    Each inference node builds upon previous nodes, creating a linear chain of reasoning that can be easily traced from start to finish.

    Prompting Approach¶

    The Chain of Thought reasoner uses specialized prompts that:

    • Request explicit step-by-step thinking
    • Encourage showing all relevant calculations
    • Guide toward checking work at critical junctures
    • Support reconsidering earlier steps when needed
    • Emphasize clarity and precision in each step

    Refinement Process¶

    When using the continueReasoning method, the Chain of Thought reasoner can:

    1. Extend the reasoning chain with additional steps
    2. Revise earlier steps that contain errors or flawed assumptions
    3. Explore alternative solution paths
    4. Add more detailed explanations for complex steps
    5. Verify the final conclusion with additional checks

    Example Reasoning Output¶

    Below is an example of the output structure produced by the Chain of Thought reasoner:

    Problem: A store has a 25% off sale. If an item originally costs $120, what is the final price after applying a 10% coupon to the already discounted price?

Step 1: Calculate the price after the 25% discount.
Discount amount = $120 × 0.25 = $30
Price after discount = $120 - $30 = $90

Step 2: Calculate the price after applying the 10% coupon to the discounted price.
Coupon discount = $90 × 0.10 = $9
Final price = $90 - $9 = $81

Solution: The final price after both the 25% sale discount and the 10% coupon is $81.

    Here's another example demonstrating more complex reasoning:

    Problem: What would be the environmental impact if all passenger cars in the US switched to electric by 2030?

Step 1: Establish key facts about the current US passenger car fleet.
- There are approximately 250 million registered passenger vehicles in the US
- The average US passenger vehicle emits about
  4.6 metric tons of CO2 per year
- The average lifespan of a car is around 12 years
- Current EV adoption is approximately 1-2% of new car sales

Step 2: Calculate current emissions from passenger vehicles.
Total annual CO2 emissions = 250 million cars × 4.6 tons CO2/car
                          = 1.15 billion tons of CO2 per year

Step 3: Consider electricity generation for EVs.
- EVs require electricity which currently comes from a mix of sources
- US electricity generation mix (approximate): 40% natural gas, 20% coal, 20% nuclear, 20% renewables
- EVs are more energy-efficient than internal combustion vehicles
- The carbon intensity of electricity varies by region

Step 4: Estimate emissions reduction.
- Direct vehicle emissions would approach zero
- Electricity generation emissions would increase
- Based on current electricity mix, EVs typically produce 60-70% less lifecycle emissions than gas vehicles
- Potential reduction = 1.15 billion × 0.65 = approximately 750 million tons CO2 annually

Step 5: Consider other environmental factors.
- Battery production has significant environmental impacts (mining, manufacturing)
- Increased demand for materials like lithium, cobalt, and rare earth elements
- Reduced air pollution in urban areas (NOx, particulates)
- Noise pollution reduction
- End-of-life battery recycling challenges and opportunities

Step 6: Assess practical constraints.
- Charging infrastructure requirements would be extensive
- Electricity grid upgrades would be necessary
- Manufacturing capacity would need dramatic scaling
- The transition timeline (by 2030) is highly aggressive
- Complete conversion by 2030 is likely unrealistic

Solution: A complete transition to EVs by 2030 would potentially reduce direct CO2 emissions by approximately 750 million tons annually based on the current electricity mix, with greater reductions as the grid becomes cleaner. However, such a rapid transition faces significant practical constraints in manufacturing capacity and infrastructure development. The environmental benefits would include substantial reductions in urban air pollution and noise, but would be partially offset by increased environmental impacts from battery production and material extraction. A more realistic timeline would involve a gradual transition extending well beyond 2030, with environmental benefits increasing as both vehicle technology and the electricity grid become cleaner.
